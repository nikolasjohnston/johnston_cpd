Qualifier 6: Evaluate Teaching Practice, Support Teaching Teams, and Engage in Continuing Professional Development

Qualifier 6 · Evaluation & CPD

I actively use evaluation data to reflect on and improve my teaching practice, and I contribute to the professional development of teaching teams within my school. My approach to evaluation is informed by a commitment to evidence-based reflection, where student feedback, peer input, and personal experience collectively shape iterative improvements to my teaching.

Engaging with Student Evaluation Data

I regularly review and reflect on Teaching Evaluation (TEBS) data across the subjects I teach.

Combined evaluation data has consistently demonstrated strong performance, with an overall mean of 5.45/6.0 for 2025.

I engage critically with areas where evaluation data identifies opportunities for improvement. For example, feedback relating to timeliness of feedback and clarity of assessment expectations (approximately 5.17–5.27/6.0 in BIOL340) has informed specific changes to my practice, including the introduction of formative checkpoints, clearer marking criteria, restructured feedback workflows and a dedicated consulation meeting tool which facilitates students booking one-on-one meetings with me.

Qualitative feedback from students has also shaped my teaching: students have noted the value of interactive elements, the clarity of structured explanations, and the supportive learning environment, while also identifying areas where communication about assessment requirements could be strengthened. I use this feedback as a direct input into subject redesign and teaching refinement.

Supporting and Mentoring Teaching Teams

As Subject Coordinator for BIOL340, I am responsible for coordinating and supporting a teaching team that includes academic staff with diverse specialisations and a technical officer. Managing this team requires deliberate coordination, clear communication, and the creation of shared resources and frameworks that allow each member to contribute effectively to a coherent student experience.

I support team coherence through several mechanisms:

  • Pre-lab orientation materials — Prior to each practical session, I prepare and share detailed materials that set out the learning objectives, expected student background, and key technical and pedagogical points. These ensure all teaching staff can orient students consistently and address questions with confidence.
  • Pre-session briefings — I hold briefings with the teaching team to discuss session goals, anticipated student challenges, and any adjustments to materials based on feedback from previous cohorts.
  • Peer mentoring — I work alongside colleagues to share pedagogical strategies — for example, discussing how the scaffolded practical structure supports students with limited computational experience, or how the block-based coding activities can be facilitated to maximise engagement.

Supporting Colleagues Through the SSCI AI Working Group

I contribute to supporting colleagues through my active role in the SSCI AI Working Group, where I have helped establish school-wide guidelines for teaching with AI and designing resilient assessments. This work has involved leading workshops and delivering presentations to academic staff across the School of Science, sharing both conceptual frameworks and practical tools developed through my scholarship. Specific contributions include:

  • Presenting the GARI (Generative AI Risk Index) framework to school colleagues, providing a shared language and structured approach for evaluating and mitigating AI-related risks in assessment design.
  • Co-developing guidelines for the ethical and pedagogically sound integration of generative AI into teaching and assessment across the school.
  • Delivering workshops on AI in research and teaching contexts, supporting colleagues in developing their own approaches to AI-responsive pedagogy.

Impact Example: GARI Pilot Consultation

The practical impact of this mentoring is illustrated by one concrete example from the GARI pilot. Following a consultation arising from the working group’s assessment review, one subject coordinator reflected on a high-risk assessment and recognised that two of the GARI framework’s built-in mitigation strategies were applicable to their context: reducing the assessment’s weighting (thereby reducing the incentive for AI misuse) and providing explicit, specific guidance to students about permissible AI use.

By implementing these two targeted adjustments, the coordinator was able to reduce the assessment’s composite risk classification from “high” to “moderate” — without requiring a full redesign. This outcome illustrates how the GARI framework functions not only as an analytical tool, but as a practical scaffold that supports educators in taking evidence-based, proportionate action.

Evidence: SSCI AI Working Group presentation materials and evaluation reports are available in the evidence folder.

Engaging in Continuing Professional Development

My engagement in CPD is driven by a recognition that effective teaching requires ongoing learning and adaptation. I have pursued professional development through multiple pathways:

  • Engagement with teaching and learning scholarship (detailed in Qualifier 5)
  • Attendance at institutional and external conferences
  • Collaborative work with colleagues on curriculum and assessment innovation
  • Sustained involvement in the SSCI AI Working Group
  • Completion of the 2024 UOW Early Career Academic Development Program

Indeed the creation of this portfolio itself has been a valuable opportunity to critically evaluate my teaching in relation to the CPD framework and identify areas for ongoing growth. Feedback from students, evaluation data, and the experience of scholarly engagement continue to shape my development as an educator.
